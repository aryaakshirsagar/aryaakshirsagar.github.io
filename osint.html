OSINT Recon

OSINT is like being a detective on the internet, but without breaking in anywhere. You’re just looking for stuff people accidentally left exposed online. Email addresses, sneaky PDFs, secret login pages the usual digital breadcrumbs, the internet’s version of “Oops, did I leave this here?”

Why Bother With OSINT?

Whenever I start a project like a CTF challenge, bug bounty, or a side investigation. I spend a good chunk of time gathering all publicly available info first. Sure, it’s not flashy, but it saves you chasing dead ends later until suddenly, you’ve got a juicy lead nobody else saw coming.

The Workflow I Follow

For me, it usually starts with what I call the “invisible footprints” stuff like:

  • Passive snooping: Google-fu and WHOIS stalking. Find out who owns the domain and when they bought it. Sometimes that’s the jackpot.
  • Going full Sherlock with theHarvester, because manually googling emails is for amateurs.
  • “Google dorking” : hunting PDFs, spreadsheets, or hidden login pages with search commands like site:target.com filetype:pdf.

Occasionally I jump into “active recon” territory, but only when I have the green light. That means poking around ports, enumerating subdomains with tools like sublist3r, or creeping on exposed devices with Shodan (because who doesn’t want to know if their neighbor’s webcam is on the internet?).
